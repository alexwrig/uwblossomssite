The Trees

A Century of Blossoms

Thirty Yoshino cherry trees line the UW Quad, a living gift whose roots reach back to a diplomatic gesture, a world war, and decades of patient stewardship by the university community.

1939
The Original Planting

Thirty Yoshino cherry trees (Prunus × yedoensis) were planted around the Liberal Arts Quadrangle in 1939, a gift facilitated through the university's growing ties with Japan. The variety, the same that lines the Tidal Basin in Washington D.C., was chosen for its spectacular single-petal white-to-blush blooms and vigorous growth in the Pacific Northwest climate.

1942 – 1945
War and Silence

Following the attack on Pearl Harbor and the forced internment of Japanese Americans, the trees became a quiet symbol of tension on campus. University staff continued to care for them through the war years, a small act of preservation during an era of profound loss for the Japanese American community, many of whom were UW students and faculty.

The Tree
Yoshino Cherry: Prunus × yedoensis

The Yoshino is a hybrid cherry believed to have originated in Tokyo in the late 19th century. Its blossoms emerge before the leaves, creating a pure canopy of white and pale pink against bare branches. Each flower has five petals and a faint almond fragrance. At peak bloom, the 30 UW trees produce an estimated 7–8 million individual flowers.

The trees are now over 85 years old, well past the typical 50-year lifespan for urban cherries, and require careful ongoing maintenance to remain healthy.

Renewal
Grafting the Next Generation

To preserve the original genetic stock, UW horticulturalists have propagated cuttings from the Quad trees through a grafting program. Young trees grown from these clippings serve as insurance against disease or storm damage, ensuring that future generations of Huskies will walk under the same blossoms, genetically identical to those planted in 1939.

Bloom Science
Why March? The Phenology of Sakura

Cherry blossom timing is driven by temperature accumulation over winter and early spring. The trees require a period of cold dormancy (chilling hours below 45°F) followed by warm spring days. Researchers use degree-day models to track heat accumulation from a base temperature, predicting bloom onset when a threshold is crossed. Seattle's maritime climate (mild winters, cool springs) consistently places UW peak bloom in the third week of March, though warming trends have gradually shifted the average earlier over the past two decades.
